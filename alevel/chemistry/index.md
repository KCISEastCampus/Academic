## 1. Atomic Structure (3.1.1)

### 1.1 Fundamental Particles
- **Protons, neutrons, electrons**: relative charge and relative mass
- An atom consists of a **nucleus** (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons

### 1.2 Mass Number and Isotopes
- **Mass number (A)** = protons + neutrons
- **Atomic number (Z)** = number of protons
- **Isotopes**: atoms of same element with different numbers of neutrons
- **Time of Flight (TOF) Mass Spectrometer**:
  1. **Ionisation**
  2. **Acceleration** (all ions gain same kinetic energy)
  3. **Ion drift**
  4. **Ion detection**
  5. **Data analysis**

### 1.3 Electron Configuration
- **Aufbau principle**: electrons fill lowest available energy levels
- Electron configurations up to $Z=36$ in terms of shells and subshells (s, p, d)
- **Ionisation energies**: provide evidence for electron configurations
- **First ionisation energy**: energy to remove 1 mole of electrons from 1 mole of gaseous atoms

## 2. Amount of Substance (3.1.2)

### 2.1 Relative Atomic and Molecular Mass
- **Relative atomic mass ($A_r$)**: compared to $^{12}$C
- **Relative molecular mass ($M_r$)**: sum of $A_r$ for all atoms in molecule
- **Relative formula mass**: used for ionic compounds

### 2.2 The Mole and Avogadro Constant
- **Avogadro constant** ($N_A$): $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles per mole
- **Mole calculations**:
  $$n = \frac{m}{M}$$
  where $n$ = moles, $m$ = mass (g), $M$ = molar mass (g/mol)
- **Solution concentration**:
  $$c = \frac{n}{V}$$
  where $c$ = concentration (mol dmâ»Â³), $V$ = volume (dmÂ³)

### 2.3 The Ideal Gas Equation
$$PV = nRT$$
- $P$ = pressure (Pa)
- $V$ = volume (mÂ³)
- $n$ = moles
- $R$ = 8.31 J Kâ»Â¹ molâ»Â¹
- $T$ = temperature (K)

**Molar volume at RTP**: 24 dmÂ³ per mole

### 2.4 Empirical and Molecular Formulas
- **Empirical formula**: simplest whole number ratio
- **Molecular formula**: actual number of atoms
- **Relationship**: molecular formula = multiple of empirical formula

### 2.5 Balanced Equations and Calculations
- **Percentage atom economy**:
  $$\frac{\text{molecular mass of desired product}}{\text{sum of molecular masses of all reactants}} \times 100$$

**Required Practical 1**: Make up a volumetric solution and carry out a simple acid-base titration

### 3.2 Nature of Covalent Bonds
- **Covalent bond**: shared pair of electrons
- **Dative covalent (co-ordinate) bond**: both electrons supplied by one atom

### 3.3 Metallic Bonding
- Attraction between delocalised electrons and positive ions in lattice

### 3.4 Bonding and Physical Properties
**Four types of crystal structure**:
1. **Ionic** (e.g., NaCl)
2. **Metallic** (e.g., Mg)
3. **Macromolecular/Giant covalent** (e.g., diamond, graphite)
4. **Molecular** (e.g., ice, iodine)

### 3.5 Shapes of Simple Molecules (VSEPR Theory)
- **Key principle**: electron pairs repel each other
- **Repulsion order**: lone pair-lone pair > lone pair-bond pair > bond pair-bond pair

### 3.6 Bond Polarity
- **Electronegativity**: ability to attract bonding pair
- **Polar bonds**: occur between atoms with different electronegativity
- **Permanent dipoles**: molecules with uneven charge distribution

### 3.7 Intermolecular Forces
**Three types**:
1. **Permanent dipole-dipole forces**
2. **Induced dipole-dipole (London dispersion) forces**
3. **Hydrogen bonding** (H bonded to N, O, or F)

**Hydrogen bonding effects**:
- Anomalous boiling points
- Low density of ice
- High solubility of small polar molecules

## 4. Periodicity (3.2.1)

### 4.1 Classification
- **s-block**: Groups 1 & 2 (valence electrons in s orbitals)
- **p-block**: Groups 13-18 (valence electrons in p orbitals)
- **d-block**: Transition metals
- **f-block**: Lanthanides and actinides

### 4.2 Physical Properties of Period 3
**Trends in atomic radius**:
- **Decreases across period** (Na â†’ Ar)
- **Reason**: Increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer

**Trends in first ionisation energy**:
- **Generally increases across period**
- **Exceptions**: 
  - Drop from Mg to Al (electron in higher energy p orbital)
  - Drop from P to S (electron pairing repulsion)

**Melting point trends**:
- **Na, Mg, Al**: increase due to stronger metallic bonding
- **Si**: very high due to giant covalent structure
- **Pâ‚„, Sâ‚ˆ, Clâ‚‚, Ar**: low due to simple molecular structures with weak intermolecular forces

## 5. Group 2 - Alkaline Earth Metals (3.2.2)

### 5.1 General Properties
- **Electronic configuration**: $ns^2$
- **Reactivity increases down group**
- All metals with metallic bonding

### 5.2 Trends Down Group

| Property | Trend | Reason |
|----------|-------|--------|
| **Atomic radius** | Increases | More electron shells |
| **First IE** | Decreases | Increased shielding and distance outweighs increased nuclear charge |
| **Melting point** | Decreases | Weaker metallic bonding (ions larger, electron cloud less effective) |

**Note**: Mg doesn't fit melting point trend perfectly due to HCP crystal structure

### 5.3 Reactions
**With water**:
- **Mg**: slow with cold water, fast with steam
  $$\mathrm{Mg(s) + H_2O(g) \rightarrow MgO(s) + H_2(g)}$$

  $$\mathrm{M(s) + 2H_2O(l) \rightarrow M(OH)_2(aq) + H_2(g)}$$

**With oxygen**:
$$\mathrm{2M(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2MO(s)}$$
- Mg: bright white flame
- Ba: apple-green flame

## 6. Energetics (3.1.4)

### 6.1 Energy Changes
- **Exothermic**: $\Delta H < 0$ (heat released)
- **Endothermic**: $\Delta H > 0$ (heat absorbed)

**Standard conditions**: 100 kPa, 298 K, 1 mol dmâ»Â³

### 6.2 Enthalpy Changes
- **Standard enthalpy of formation ($\Delta H_f^\circ$)**: formation of 1 mole from elements
- **Standard enthalpy of combustion ($\Delta H_c^\circ$)**: complete combustion of 1 mole in oxygen

### 6.3 Calorimetry
$$Q = mc\Delta T$$
- $Q$ = heat energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (g)
- $c$ = specific heat capacity (J gâ»Â¹ Kâ»Â¹)
- $\Delta T$ = temperature change (K)

**For water**: $c = 4.18$ J gâ»Â¹ Kâ»Â¹

**Calculating $\Delta H$**:
$$\Delta H = \frac{Q}{n} \quad \text{(convert to kJ molâ»Â¹)}$$

**Required Practical 2**: Measure an enthalpy change

### 6.4 Hess's Law
- **Total enthalpy change independent of reaction pathway**

**Method 1 - Using $\Delta H_f^\circ$**:
$$\Delta H_{reaction} = \sum \Delta H_f^\circ(products) - \sum \Delta H_f^\circ(reactants)$$

**Method 2 - Using $\Delta H_c^\circ$**:
$$\Delta H_{reaction} = \sum \Delta H_c^\circ(reactants) - \sum \Delta H_c^\circ(products)$$

### 6.5 Bond Enthalpies
**Mean bond enthalpy**: average energy to break 1 mole of bonds

**Calculating $\Delta H$**:
$$\Delta H = \sum(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum(\text{bonds formed})$$

- **Breaking bonds**: endothermic (+ve)
- **Forming bonds**: exothermic (-ve)
