## 1. ‚öõÔ∏è Atomic Structure (3.1.1)

### 1.1 Fundamental Particles
* An atom consists of a dense **nucleus** (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in orbitals.
* The **Relative Mass** and **Charge** of fundamental particles are crucial:

| Particle | Relative Charge | Relative Mass | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Proton** | $+1$ | $1$ | Nucleus |
| **Neutron** | $0$ | $1$ | Nucleus |
| **Electron** | $-1$ | $\approx \frac{1}{1840}$ | Shells/Orbitals |

### 1.2 Mass Number and Isotopes
* **Mass number ($A$)** = protons + neutrons.
* **Atomic number ($Z$)** = number of protons (defines the element).
* **Isotopes**: Atoms of the same element (same $Z$) but with different numbers of **neutrons** (different $A$).
* **Time of Flight (TOF) Mass Spectrometer** üöÄ: Used to determine relative atomic/molecular mass and isotopic abundance.
    1.  **Ionisation**: Convert sample into **positive gas-phase ions** (usually $\text{X}^+$).
    2.  **Acceleration**: Ions accelerate to the same **kinetic energy** ($KE$). ($KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$)
    3.  **Ion drift**: Ions travel through a vacuum; **lighter ions (smaller $m$) travel faster (larger $v$)** and reach the detector first.
    4.  **Ion detection**: Detector records the arrival of ions.
    5.  **Data analysis**: Flight time is used to calculate the mass-to-charge ratio ($m/z$) and relative abundance.


![Image of time of flight mass spectrometer diagram](/assets/img/ToF-simple-diagram.png)


### 1.3 Electron Configuration
* **Aufbau principle**: Electrons fill the **lowest available energy levels** first.
* **Hund's Rule**: Electrons fill degenerate orbitals (like $p$ orbitals) singly before pairing up.
* Electron configurations up to $Z=36$ in terms of shells and subshells ($s, p, d$).
* **Ionisation energies**: Successive IEs show evidence for electron shells and subshells (large jumps indicate moving to a new, inner shell).
* **First ionisation energy**: Energy required to remove $1$ mole of electrons from $1$ mole of **gaseous atoms** to form $1$ mole of gaseous $1+$ ions.
    $$\mathrm{X(g)} \rightarrow \mathrm{X^+(g)} + \mathrm{e^-}$$

## 2. ‚öñÔ∏è Amount of Substance (3.1.2)

### 2.1 Relative Atomic and Molecular Mass
* **Relative atomic mass ($A_r$)**: Weighted mean mass of an atom compared to $\frac{1}{12}$ the mass of a $^{12}\text{C}$ atom.
* **Relative molecular mass ($M_r$)**: Sum of $A_r$ for all atoms in a molecule.
* **Relative formula mass**: Used for **ionic compounds**.

### 2.2 The Mole and Avogadro Constant
* **Avogadro constant** ($N_A$): $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles per mole.
* **Mole calculations (Mass)**:
¬† $$n = \frac{m}{M}$$
¬† where $n$ = moles, $m$ = mass (g), $M$ = molar mass (g/mol)
* **Solution concentration**:
¬† $$c = \frac{n}{V}$$
¬† where $c$ = concentration ($\text{mol dm}^{-3}$), $V$ = volume ($\text{dm}^3$)
> **Unit Note**: $1 \text{ dm}^3 = 1000 \text{ cm}^3$.

### 2.3 The Ideal Gas Equation
$$PV = nRT$$
* $P$ = pressure (Pa) **($1 \text{ kPa} = 1000 \text{ Pa}$)**
* $V$ = volume (m¬≥) **($1 \text{ dm}^3 = 0.001 \text{ m}^3$)**
* $n$ = moles
* $R$ = $8.31$ J K‚Åª¬π mol‚Åª¬π (The Ideal Gas Constant)
* $T$ = temperature (K) **($\text{K} = ^\circ \text{C} + 273$)**

**Molar volume at RTP**: $24$ $\text{dm}^3$ per mole.

### 2.4 Empirical and Molecular Formulas
* **Empirical formula**: The **simplest whole number ratio** of atoms in a compound.
* **Molecular formula**: The **actual number of atoms** of each element in a molecule.
* **Relationship**: Molecular formula = $k \times$ Empirical formula ($k$ is an integer)

### 2.5 Balanced Equations and Calculations
* **Percentage atom economy**: Measures the efficiency of a reaction (how much reactant is converted into useful product).
¬† $$\frac{\text{molecular mass of desired product}}{\text{sum of molecular masses of all reactants}} \times 100$$
* High atom economy ‚ôªÔ∏è is central to green chemistry.

## 3. üîó Bonding (3.1.3)

### 3.1 Ionic Bonding
* Strong **electrostatic attraction** between oppositely charged ions in a lattice.
* **Prediction**: Group 1/2/13 metals form cations, Group 15/16/17 non-metals form anions.
* **Common compound ions**: sulfate ($\text{SO}_4^{2-}$), hydroxide ($\text{OH}^-$), nitrate ($\text{NO}_3^-$), carbonate ($\text{CO}_3^{2-}$), ammonium ($\text{NH}_4^+$).

### 3.2 Nature of Covalent Bonds
* **Covalent bond**: Shared pair of electrons.
* **Dative covalent (co-ordinate) bond**: Both shared electrons are supplied by **one** atom.

### 3.3 Metallic Bonding
* Attraction between a lattice of **positive ions** and a 'sea' of **delocalised electrons**.

### 3.4 Bonding and Physical Properties
**Four types of crystal structure** (and their properties):
1.  **Ionic** (e.g., $\text{NaCl}$): High M.P., conducts only when molten/dissolved.
2.  **Metallic** (e.g., $\text{Mg}$): High M.P., malleable, conductive.
3.  **Macromolecular/Giant covalent** (e.g., diamond, $\text{Si}$): Extremely high M.P., hard.
4.  **Molecular** (e.g., ice, $\text{I}_2$): Low M.P., weak IMFs.

### 3.5 Shapes of Simple Molecules (VSEPR Theory)
* **Key principle**: Electron pairs (both lone pairs and bonding pairs) repel each other to achieve **maximum separation**.
* **Repulsion order**: lone pair-lone pair > lone pair-bond pair > bond pair-bond pair.


![Image of VSEPR molecule shapes and bond angles](/assets/img/vsepr-shapes-angles-lone-pairs-a-level-chemistry-1720x2247.png)


**Common shapes**:

| Electron Pairs | Bonding Pairs | Lone Pairs | Shape | Example | Bond Angle |
|----------------|---------------|------------|-------|---------|------------|
| 2 | 2 | 0 | **Linear** | $\text{CO}_2$ | $180^\circ$ |
| 3 | 3 | 0 | **Trigonal planar** | $\text{BF}_3$ | $120^\circ$ |
| 3 | 2 | 1 | **Bent/V-shaped** | $\text{SO}_2$ | $<120^\circ$ |
| 4 | 4 | 0 | **Tetrahedral** | $\text{CH}_4$ | $109.5^\circ$ |
| 4 | 3 | 1 | **Trigonal pyramidal** | $\text{NH}_3$ | $107^\circ$ |
| 4 | 2 | 2 | **Bent/V-shaped** | $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ | $104.5^\circ$ |

### 3.6 Bond Polarity
* **Electronegativity**: The ability of an atom to attract the **bonding pair of electrons** in a covalent bond.
* **Polar bonds**: Occur between atoms with a significant $\Delta\text{EN}$.
* **Permanent dipoles**: Molecules with a net uneven charge distribution (dipoles don't cancel).

### 3.7 Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)
**Hierarchy of strength (Weakest to Strongest)**:
1.  **Induced dipole-dipole (London Dispersion Forces)**: Temporary, fluctuating dipoles in all molecules.
2.  **Permanent dipole-dipole forces**: Attraction between permanent dipoles in polar molecules.
3.  **Hydrogen bonding** üí°: Strongest IMF, occurs when $\text{H}$ is bonded directly to highly electronegative **$\text{N}$, $\text{O}$, or $\text{F}$**.

**Hydrogen bonding effects**:
* **Anomalous properties of water**: High boiling point, high surface tension.
* **Low density of ice**: Open lattice structure due to H-bonds.

## 4. üß≠ Periodicity (3.2.1)

### 4.1 Classification
* **s-block**: Groups 1 & 2 (valence electrons in $s$ orbitals).
* **p-block**: Groups 13-18 (valence electrons in $p$ orbitals).
* **d-block**: Transition metals.
* **f-block**: Lanthanides and actinides.

### 4.2 Physical Properties of Period 3
**Trends in atomic radius**:
* **Decreases across period** ($\text{Na} \to \text{Ar}$).
* **Reason**: Increasing nuclear charge with electrons entering the **same shell** (minimal shielding change), pulling them closer.

**Trends in first ionisation energy**:
* **Generally increases across period**.
* **Exceptions (Drops)**:
    * **$\text{Mg} \to \text{Al}$**: The electron is removed from the $3p$ orbital, which is **higher in energy** and slightly **shielded** by the $3s$ electrons.
    * **$\text{P} \to \text{S}$**: $\text{S}$ has a paired electron in one $3p$ orbital, leading to **electron-electron repulsion**, making it easier to remove.

**Melting point trends**:
* **$\text{Na}, \text{Mg}, \text{Al}$**: Increase due to stronger metallic bonding (more delocalised electrons and smaller ion size).
* **$\text{Si}$**: Very high due to **giant covalent structure**.
* **$\text{P}_4, \text{S}_8, \text{Cl}_2, \text{Ar}$**: Low due to **simple molecular structures** held by weak IMFs.

## 5. üëë Group 2 - Alkaline Earth Metals (3.2.2)

### 5.1 General Properties
* **Electronic configuration**: $ns^2$.
* **Reactivity increases down group** (easier to lose $2$ electrons).
* All metals with metallic bonding.

### 5.2 Trends Down Group

| Property | Trend | Reason |
|----------|-------|--------|
| **Atomic radius** | Increases | More electron shells |
| **First IE** | Decreases | Increased shielding and distance outweighs increased nuclear charge |
| **Melting point** | Decreases | Weaker metallic bonding (ions are larger, delocalised electron density is less effective) |

**Note**: $\text{Mg}$'s melting point is an anomaly due to its specific crystal structure (HCP).

### 5.3 Reactions
**With water**:
* **$\text{Mg}$**: Slow with cold water, **fast with steam** (to form $\text{MgO}$).
    $$\mathrm{Mg(s) + H_2O(g) \rightarrow MgO(s) + H_2(g)}$$
* **$\text{Ca}, \text{Sr}, \text{Ba}$**: Increasingly vigorous with cold water (to form the hydroxide, $\text{M}(\text{OH})_2$).
    $$\mathrm{M(s) + 2H_2O(l) \rightarrow M(OH)_2(aq) + H_2(g)}$$

**With oxygen**:
$$\mathrm{2M(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2MO(s)}$$
* $\text{Mg}$: **Bright white flame**.
* $\text{Ba}$: **Apple-green flame**.

### 5.4 Compounds
**Hydroxides solubility**:
* **Increases down group**.
    * $\text{Mg}(\text{OH})_2$: Sparingly soluble (**used in indigestion tablets as an antacid**).
    * $\text{Ca}(\text{OH})_2$: Slightly soluble (**used in agriculture to neutralise acid soils**).
* **Sulfates solubility**:
* **Decreases down group**.
    * $\text{BaSO}_4$: Insoluble (**used in barium meals for X-rays** as it absorbs them, but is non-toxic because it doesn't dissolve).

### 5.5 Tests and Uses
**Sulfate test** (Testing for $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$ ions):
1.  Acidify sample (e.g., with $\text{HNO}_3$ or $\text{HCl}$) to remove any carbonate ions that could give a false positive.
2.  Add $\text{BaCl}_2$ solution.
3.  White precipitate ($\text{BaSO}_4$) = sulfate ions present.
    $$\mathrm{Ba^{2+}(aq) + SO_4^{2-}(aq) \rightarrow BaSO_4(s)}$$

**Titanium extraction**: A key use of Group 2 metals ($\text{Mg}$ is the reducing agent).
$$\mathrm{TiO_2 + 2C + 2Cl_2 \rightarrow TiCl_4 + 2CO}$$
$$\mathrm{TiCl_4 + 2Mg \rightarrow Ti + 2MgCl_2}$$

**Flue gas desulfurisation**: Removing harmful $\text{SO}_2$ from power station emissions.
$$\mathrm{CaO + SO_2 \rightarrow CaSO_3}$$

## 6. üî• Energetics (3.1.4)

### 6.1 Energy Changes
* **Exothermic**: $\Delta H < 0$ (heat **released** to surroundings, temperature increases).
* **Endothermic**: $\Delta H > 0$ (heat **absorbed** from surroundings, temperature decreases).

**Standard conditions** ($\circ$): $100$ kPa, $298$ K ($25^\circ \text{C}$), $1 \text{ mol dm}^{-3}$ concentration.

### 6.2 Enthalpy Changes
* **Standard enthalpy of formation ($\Delta H_f^\circ$)**: Enthalpy change when $1$ mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
* **Standard enthalpy of combustion ($\Delta H_c^\circ$)**: Enthalpy change when $1$ mole of a substance undergoes **complete combustion** in excess oxygen under standard conditions.

### 6.3 Calorimetry
$$Q = mc\Delta T$$
* $Q$ = heat energy (J)
* $m$ = mass of substance heated (g)
* $c$ = specific heat capacity ($\text{J g}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$)
* $\Delta T$ = temperature change (K or $^\circ \text{C}$)

**For water**: $c = 4.18$ J g‚Åª¬π K‚Åª¬π

**Calculating $\Delta H$ (molar enthalpy)**:
$$\Delta H = -\frac{Q}{n} \quad \text{(convert to kJ mol‚Åª¬π)}$$
> **Note**: The negative sign is crucial to reflect the sign convention ($\Delta H$ is negative for exothermic reactions, which produce heat $Q$).

**Required Practical 2** üå°Ô∏è: Measuring an enthalpy change.

### 6.4 Hess's Law
* **Hess's Law**: The **total enthalpy change** for a reaction is independent of the reaction pathway taken.

**Method 1 - Using $\Delta H_f^\circ$**:
$$\Delta H_{reaction} = \sum \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta H_f^\circ(\text{reactants})$$

**Method 2 - Using $\Delta H_c^\circ$**:
$$\Delta H_{reaction} = \sum \Delta H_c^\circ(\text{reactants}) - \sum \Delta H_c^\circ(\text{products})$$

### 6.5 Bond Enthalpies
* **Mean bond enthalpy**: Average energy required to break $1$ mole of a specific bond in gaseous molecules.
* **Calculating $\Delta H$**:
$$\Delta H = \sum(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum(\text{bonds formed})$$
* **Breaking bonds**: Requires energy (Endothermic, **+ve**).
* **Forming bonds**: Releases energy (Exothermic, **-ve**).
